# Should We Pivot from B2B SaaS to B2C? Analysis and Recommendation

## Recommendation: No. Do not pivot. Consider a hybrid expansion instead.

---

## Current Position Summary

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| ARR | $2M |
| Enterprise Customers | 200 |
| Average Contract Value (ACV) | $50K/yr |
| Team Size | 30 |
| Runway | 18 months |
| Consumer Waitlist | 10K signups (Product Hunt) |

---

## Why a Full Pivot Is the Wrong Move

### 1. You have real revenue with strong unit economics

$50K ACV across 200 customers is a solid B2B foundation. These are paying enterprise customers with validated willingness to spend. Abandoning this for an unproven consumer model would mean walking away from $2M in recurring revenue with no guarantee of replacing it.

### 2. Consumer waitlist signups are not revenue

10K waitlist signups from Product Hunt are an interest signal, not a demand signal. Conversion rates from waitlist to paid consumer users are typically 2-5%. Even at an optimistic $10/mo consumer price point with 5% conversion, that yields roughly $60K ARR -- a fraction of what a single enterprise customer pays. You would need orders of magnitude more consumer users to match your current revenue.

### 3. B2C economics are fundamentally different and harder

- **Customer acquisition cost (CAC):** B2C project management is a brutally competitive market (Notion, Todoist, Trello, Asana free tier, Monday.com). You would be competing against well-funded incumbents with massive consumer brand recognition.
- **Lifetime value (LTV):** Consumer users churn faster, pay less, and require more support at scale.
- **Infrastructure costs:** Serving potentially millions of free/low-cost users requires significant infrastructure investment your current runway may not support.
- **Team composition:** Your 30-person team is presumably built for B2B -- enterprise sales, customer success, implementation. B2C requires growth marketing, product-led growth expertise, viral loops, and consumer UX design. This is essentially rebuilding the company.

### 4. 18 months of runway is not enough for a pivot of this magnitude

A B2B-to-B2C pivot typically takes 12-24 months just to find product-market fit in the new segment. With 18 months of runway, you would be racing the clock with no safety net. If the consumer bet does not work, you will have cannibalized your enterprise business and have nothing to fall back on.

---

## What the Waitlist Signal Actually Tells You

The Product Hunt traction suggests your product has qualities that resonate beyond the enterprise -- likely good design, clear UX, or a compelling workflow. This is valuable information, but the right response is not to abandon your core business. It is to explore the opportunity without jeopardizing what is working.

---

## Recommended Path: Hybrid Expansion (Product-Led Growth into SMB/Mid-Market)

### Phase 1: Protect and grow the enterprise base (Months 1-6)
- Continue investing in enterprise sales and customer success.
- Target increasing ACV to $60-75K through upselling and expansion revenue.
- Aim to grow from 200 to 280-300 enterprise customers.
- This alone could push ARR toward $3-4M and extend runway or position for fundraising.

### Phase 2: Launch a self-serve tier (Months 3-9)
- Introduce a lower-cost self-serve plan ($20-50/user/mo) targeting SMBs and prosumers.
- Use the waitlist as your initial user base for this tier.
- This is not a pivot -- it is a new go-to-market motion layered on top of your existing business.
- Build a small (2-3 person) growth pod to own this motion without disrupting the core team.

### Phase 3: Evaluate and decide (Months 9-18)
- After 6+ months of self-serve data, you will have real metrics: conversion rates, retention, willingness to pay, CAC, and LTV.
- If self-serve shows strong economics, you can gradually shift more resources toward it.
- If it underperforms, you have lost very little and your enterprise business is stronger than ever.

---

## Key Risks to Monitor

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Self-serve tier cannibalizes enterprise deals | Gate enterprise features (SSO, audit logs, advanced permissions, SLAs) behind the enterprise plan |
| Team loses focus trying to serve two segments | Dedicate a small, separate pod to self-serve; do not split the core team's attention |
| Waitlist users have fundamentally different needs than enterprise | Validate with user interviews before building; the waitlist may actually want a simpler product, not a cheaper version of the enterprise one |
| Runway pressure forces premature decisions | Prioritize enterprise revenue growth first; self-serve is an experiment, not a bet-the-company move |

---

## Bottom Line

You have a working B2B business generating $2M ARR with strong ACV. A full pivot to B2C based on 10K waitlist signups would be trading proven revenue for speculation in one of the most competitive consumer software categories. The waitlist signal is worth exploring, but through a low-risk self-serve expansion -- not a company-redefining pivot. Protect what is working, experiment carefully, and let the data guide larger strategic shifts.
