# Should We Launch a Free Tier? Strategic Analysis

## Current Position

- **Product price:** $99/mo
- **Paying customers:** 5,000
- **MRR:** $60,000
- **MoM growth:** 15%
- **Situation:** Competitor just launched a free tier and is seeing high signup volume

## Recommendation: Do Not Launch a Free Tier Right Now

A reactive free tier launch would likely damage your business more than it helps. Here is the reasoning.

---

## Why the Numbers Argue Against It

### Your growth is already strong

15% MoM growth is exceptional. At that rate, you double roughly every five months. Projected out:

| Timeline | Customers | MRR |
|----------|-----------|-----|
| Today | 5,000 | $60K |
| 6 months | ~11,500 | ~$138K |
| 12 months | ~26,700 | ~$320K |

Introducing a free tier to "respond" to a competitor risks disrupting a growth engine that is already compounding well.

### The unit economics are punishing

Free users cost money to serve (infrastructure, support, onboarding) but generate zero revenue. At $99/mo per paying customer, your average revenue is high enough that even a small dilution in conversion focus can be costly. Most SaaS companies with free tiers see conversion rates of 2-5% from free to paid. To replace the revenue impact of even modest churn or distraction, you would need enormous free-user volume.

### Competitor signup volume is not competitor revenue

Signups are a vanity metric. Your competitor is likely burning cash to acquire users who may never convert. High signup volume after a free launch is almost guaranteed -- it says nothing about whether those users will pay, retain, or generate positive LTV. Do not confuse their top-of-funnel noise with a threat to your business.

---

## The Real Risks of a Free Tier

1. **Brand and positioning erosion.** Moving from a $99/mo product to "free" signals to the market that the product may not be worth $99. It can undermine the perceived value for your existing 5,000 paying customers.

2. **Support and infrastructure burden.** Free users consume resources disproportionate to their value. Support tickets, server costs, and engineering time for free-tier edge cases will pull resources away from improving the paid product.

3. **Conversion complexity.** You now need a paywall strategy, feature-gating logic, upgrade nudges, and a dedicated growth team to optimize free-to-paid conversion. This is a major product and engineering investment, not a marketing switch you flip.

4. **Cannibalization.** Some percentage of prospects who would have paid $99 will now start on free and either never convert or take much longer to do so.

5. **Team focus dilution.** Your team will split attention between serving free users and delivering value to paying customers. At 5K customers and $60K MRR, you are still at a stage where focus is your most important asset.

---

## What to Do Instead

### Short-term responses (next 30 days)

- **Monitor, do not react.** Track whether the competitor's free launch actually impacts your pipeline, trial starts, or close rates. If your metrics hold steady, the threat is theoretical.
- **Strengthen your trial experience.** If you have a free trial, make it better -- shorter time-to-value, better onboarding, more activation touchpoints. A well-executed 14-day trial of a $99 product beats a permanent free tier.
- **Sharpen positioning.** Lean into what makes your product worth $99. Case studies, ROI calculators, and customer testimonials that quantify value. Position against the competitor: "They're free because they're trying to figure out their product. We charge because ours already works."

### Medium-term moves (next 90 days)

- **Consider a limited free offering only if data supports it.** If you see measurable pipeline impact from the competitor's move (not just anxiety about it), consider a narrowly scoped free plan designed purely as a lead-generation tool -- heavily limited, clearly positioned as a stepping stone. Think "free for 1 user, 1 project, limited features" rather than a generous free tier.
- **Invest in content and community.** PLG growth does not require a free product. Developer docs, templates, open-source tools adjacent to your product, and community engagement can drive awareness and inbound without giving away the core product.
- **Double down on expansion revenue.** At 5K customers, there is likely significant upsell and cross-sell opportunity. Adding seats, premium features, or usage-based pricing tiers for existing customers may be a higher-ROI growth lever than chasing free signups.

### Decision trigger for revisiting

Revisit the free tier question if any of the following occur:
- MoM growth drops below 8% for two consecutive months
- Win rates against the competitor decline by more than 10 percentage points
- Pipeline volume drops more than 20% quarter-over-quarter

---

## Summary

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Current growth trajectory | Strong -- 15% MoM is top-decile |
| Competitive threat severity | Uncertain -- signup volume is not revenue |
| Free tier ROI | Likely negative in the short/medium term |
| Opportunity cost | High -- diverts focus from paying customers |
| Recommended action | Hold position, monitor data, strengthen trial and positioning |

Do not let a competitor's tactical move push you into a strategic mistake. Your 15% MoM growth at $99/mo is a position of strength. Protect it.
